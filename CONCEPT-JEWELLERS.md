## 1. Vision

**T2C** is a lightweight, static, single-page web application that guides Australian jewellers and dealers in precious metals, stones and products through their new Anti-Money Laundering / Counter-Terrorism Financing (AML/CTF) obligations under the reformed AML/CTF Act (Tranche 2), effective **1 July 2026**.

It is **not** legal advice. It is a practical, step-by-step guidance tool built entirely from publicly available AUSTRAC materials, including the official **Jeweller Program Starter Kit** released 30 January 2026.

> **ðŸ“Ž AUSTRAC Source:** [Starter Kit Media Release](https://www.austrac.gov.au/news-and-media/media-release/austrac-backs-newly-regulated-sectors-release-amlctf-program-starter-kits) | [Dealers in Precious Metals, Stones and Products Guidance Hub](https://www.austrac.gov.au/reforms/sector-specific-guidance/dealers-precious-metals-stones-and-products-guidance)

The tool mirrors the structure of AUSTRAC's own starter kit â€” **Customise â†’ Implement â†’ Maintain & Review** â€” but wraps it in an interactive, user-friendly interface with checklists, wizards, template forms, and plain-English explanations.

**Key AUSTRAC insight:** AUSTRAC CEO Brendan Thomas stated that *"for most customers who are individuals posing a low to medium risk, compliance generally boils down to just three forms"* â€” onboarding, initial CDD, and ongoing monitoring. AUSTRAC has also developed a **streamlined compliance process** specifically for jewellers dealing with low-to-medium risk individual customers using physical currency.

**Who the starter kit is designed for:** The AUSTRAC starter kit is specifically designed for Tranche 2 entities who, from 1 July 2026, are for the first time subject to AML/CTF legislation. It is intended for typical small jewellery businesses. Larger or more complex dealers may need to supplement the starter kit with additional tailored compliance measures.

**Unique to jewellers â€” regulation options:** Unlike other Tranche 2 sectors, jewellers may be able to **avoid regulation entirely** by choosing not to accept physical currency or virtual assets for transactions of $10,000 or more. AUSTRAC provides 4 regulation options depending on your business model (see Section 3.4).

## 2. Problem Statement

From **1 July 2026**, businesses that buy or sell precious metals, stones or products for $10,000 or more in physical currency and/or virtual assets will be required to:

- Enrol with AUSTRAC as a reporting entity (within 28 days of providing a designated service â€” by **29 July 2026**)
- Develop and maintain an AML/CTF program (risk assessment, policies, processes)
- Appoint an AML/CTF compliance officer
- Conduct initial and ongoing customer due diligence (CDD)
- Screen for Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs) and Targeted Financial Sanctions (TFS)
- Report suspicious matters (SMRs) and threshold transactions (TTRs) to AUSTRAC
- Conduct personnel due diligence on staff in AML/CTF roles
- Keep records for 7 years
- Train staff on ML/TF/PF risks and obligations
- Conduct internal reviews and independent evaluations (at least every 3 years)

Most small jewellers and precious goods dealers have **zero experience** with AML/CTF compliance. AUSTRAC's 2024 National Risk Assessment rated the **retail jewellery sector as a HIGH money laundering risk** in Australia. Precious metals and stones are among the most at-risk goods for money laundering because they can be bought anonymously with cash, are easy to conceal and transport, difficult to trace, and easy to convert back to legitimate funds.

**T2C bridges that gap** by translating AUSTRAC's requirements into plain-English, actionable guidance with interactive tools â€” all for free, with no data leaving the user's browser.

## 3. Who Is Regulated (and Who Is Not)

> **ðŸ“Ž AUSTRAC Source:** [Precious Metals, Stones and Products Services (Reform)](https://www.austrac.gov.au/amlctf-reform/reforms-guidance/before-you-start/new-industries-and-services-be-regulated-reform/precious-metals-stones-and-products-services-reform) | [Regulation Options for Dealers](https://www.austrac.gov.au/reforms/sector-specific-guidance/dealers-precious-metals-stones-and-products-guidance/regulation-options-dealers-precious-metals-stones-and-products)

### 3.1 The Designated Service

The reforms capture **one category** of designated service for precious goods dealers:

| Designated Service | When It Applies |
|---|---|
| **Buying or selling precious metals, stones or products** â€” in the course of carrying on a business | When the purchase or sale: (1) is valued at **$10,000 or greater**, (2) involves a **single transaction or linked transactions**, and (3) is made using **physical currency, virtual assets, or a combination** at or above the $10,000 threshold |

**Critical distinction:** If these items are bought or sold using **only** a debit/credit card or bank transfer, the business is **NOT** providing a designated service.

### 3.2 What Are "Precious Metals, Stones and Products"?

> **ðŸ“Ž AUSTRAC Source:** [Precious Metals, Stones and Products Services (Reform)](https://www.austrac.gov.au/amlctf-reform/reforms-guidance/before-you-start/new-industries-and-services-be-regulated-reform/precious-metals-stones-and-products-services-reform)

| Category | Items Included |
|---|---|
| **Precious Metals** | Gold, silver, platinum, iridium, osmium, palladium, rhodium, ruthenium, or an alloy with at least 2% weight of any of these (manufactured or unmanufactured) |
| **Precious Stones** | Substances of gem quality with market-recognised beauty, rarity and value â€” including (not limited to): beryl, corundum, diamond, garnet, jadeite jade, opal, pearl, topaz. Applies whether synthetic, natural, or man-made. |
| **Precious Products** | Items made of, containing, or having attached precious metals or stones: jewellery, watches, items of personal adornment (belts, hair clips), goldsmith/silversmith wares (tableware, trophies, goblets, ornaments) |

**Determining if a substance is a precious stone:** Gem quality means colour, cut, clarity or carat can be assessed/graded. Beauty, rarity and value require the existence of a market for the substance.

**Note:** Bullion is a **separate** designated service regulated independently. This guidance does not cover bullion dealers.

### 3.3 What Is Exempt

| Scenario | Status |
|---|---|
| Sales paid entirely by bank transfer, debit card, or credit card | **Exempt** â€” not a designated service (no physical currency or virtual assets involved) |
| Sales under $10,000 in physical currency / virtual assets (with no linked transactions) | **Exempt** â€” below threshold |
| Costume jewellery with no precious metals or stones | **Exempt** â€” not a precious product |
| Bullion dealing | Regulated separately under bullion dealer designated services |
| Watch repair / jewellery repair only (no sales) | **Exempt** â€” not buying or selling precious products |

### 3.4 Regulation Options â€” UNIQUE TO JEWELLERS

> **ðŸ“Ž AUSTRAC Source:** [Regulation Options for Dealers in Precious Metals, Stones and Products](https://www.austrac.gov.au/reforms/sector-specific-guidance/dealers-precious-metals-stones-and-products-guidance/regulation-options-dealers-precious-metals-stones-and-products)

AUSTRAC provides **four regulation options** depending on your business model:

| Option | Description | AML/CTF Obligations |
|---|---|---|
| **Option 1: Don't accept physical currency or virtual assets** | If you don't accept cash or crypto payments at all, you are **not regulated** | **None** â€” no AML/CTF program required |
| **Option 2: Accept cash/virtual assets under $10,000 per transaction** | You must monitor for linked or structured transactions that could exceed $10,000 | **Limited** â€” need clear policies and monitoring for linked transactions. If a $10,000+ transaction occurs, you must have a program or face penalties. |
| **Option 3: Accept cash/virtual assets but limit customer types** | Use AUSTRAC's **streamlined compliance process** for low/medium risk individual customers only | **Streamlined** â€” refer physical currency transactions to store owner/manager who follows simplified process |
| **Option 4: Accept cash/virtual assets from all customer types** | Full AML/CTF program required for wider range of customers including entities, high-risk, foreign | **Full** â€” implement complete AML/CTF program using starter kit |

**Interactive Decision Aid:** The tool should include a wizard that helps jewellers determine which option applies to their business and what level of compliance they need.

### 3.5 Linked Transactions

> **ðŸ“Ž AUSTRAC Source:** [Precious Metals, Stones and Products Services (Reform)](https://www.austrac.gov.au/amlctf-reform/reforms-guidance/before-you-start/new-industries-and-services-be-regulated-reform/precious-metals-stones-and-products-services-reform)

The $10,000 threshold applies not just to single transactions but to **transactions that are linked or appear to be linked**.

**Examples of linked transactions:**
- A customer deliberately splits a larger transaction into smaller payments below $10,000 to avoid AML/CTF obligations (**structuring â€” this is a criminal offence**)
- Multiple payments made under a single invoice
- A single payment made for multiple invoices
- Payments made on the same date
- Lay-by payments or instalments

**Instalment example from AUSTRAC:**
A customer purchases a single item worth $15,000 and pays in three instalments:
- Day 1: $5,000 in cash
- Day 2: $5,000 in cash
- Day 3: $5,000 in virtual assets

Each transaction is under $10,000, but they are linked â€” the business begins providing a designated service on **Day 2** (when cumulative value exceeds $10,000).

**Mixed transactions:** If a transaction includes both regulated items (precious products) and unregulated items (e.g., leather shoes), only the regulated items count towards the $10,000 threshold.

### 7.8 Customer Due Diligence (CDD) â€” EXPANDED

> **ðŸ“Ž AUSTRAC Source:** [Step 2: Use Your Jeweller Program](https://www.austrac.gov.au/reforms/sector-specific-guidance/dealers-precious-metals-stones-and-products-guidance/jeweller-program-starter-kit/step-2-use-your-jeweller-program) | [Examples of Dealing with Customers](https://www.austrac.gov.au/reforms/sector-specific-guidance/dealers-precious-metals-stones-and-products-guidance/jeweller-program-starter-kit/jeweller-program-starter-kit-examples-dealing-customers)

**Purpose:** Provide clear, step-by-step guidance on how to identify and verify customers for each entity type, including when to apply enhanced or ongoing due diligence.

**Overarching Principle:** You must conduct initial CDD **before providing a designated service** (i.e., before completing a purchase or sale of precious goods for $10,000+ in cash/virtual assets). If CDD cannot be completed satisfactorily, you should consider whether to decline or cease providing the service.

Accepting payment through alternative means when you hold a suspicion **could make you complicit in criminal conduct**.

#### 7.8.0a Who Is Your Customer?

> **ðŸ“Ž AUSTRAC Source:** [Precious Metals, Stones and Products Services (Reform)](https://www.austrac.gov.au/amlctf-reform/reforms-guidance/before-you-start/new-industries-and-services-be-regulated-reform/precious-metals-stones-and-products-services-reform)

Your customer is the **buyer or the seller** (as the case may be) in the transaction. If you are selling precious goods, the buyer is your customer. If you are buying precious goods (e.g., buying scrap jewellery), the seller is your customer.

A Politically Exposed Person is someone who holds or has held a prominent public position, domestically or internationally. This includes heads of state, government ministers, senior government officials, military leaders, judiciary members, senior executives of state-owned enterprises, and their **family members** and **close associates**.

**AUSTRAC requirement for jewellers: You must always treat foreign PEPs as high-risk customers.**

**What Is TFS?**
Targeted Financial Sanctions against specific individuals and entities. It is illegal to provide services to sanctioned persons.

**Screening Process:**
1. Ask the customer whether they are a PEP or associated with a PEP
2. Check the Australian Government's **Consolidated List** of sanctioned persons/entities
3. Document the screening result
4. If a PEP is identified â†’ apply EDD (Section 7.8.6)
5. If a sanctioned person is identified â†’ **do not proceed** and seek legal advice

**Resources Linked:**
- DFAT Consolidated List: [https://www.dfat.gov.au/international-relations/security/sanctions/consolidated-list](https://www.dfat.gov.au/international-relations/security/sanctions/consolidated-list)

### 7.10 Reporting to AUSTRAC â€” EXPANDED

> **ðŸ“Ž AUSTRAC Source:** [Suspicious Matter Reports (Reform)](https://www.austrac.gov.au/amlctf-reform/reforms-guidance/amlctf-program-reform/reporting-austrac-reform/suspicious-matter-reports-reform) | [Threshold Transaction Reports (Reform)](https://www.austrac.gov.au/amlctf-reform/reforms-guidance/amlctf-program-reform/reporting-austrac-reform/threshold-transaction-reports-reform) | [Reporting to AUSTRAC (Reform)](https://www.austrac.gov.au/amlctf-reform/reforms-guidance/amlctf-program-reform/reporting-austrac-reform)

**Purpose:** Explain the types of reports, when to file them, how to file them, and the tipping-off prohibition.

#### 7.10.1 Suspicious Matter Reports (SMRs)

**When You Must File:**
An SMR must be submitted if you suspect, on **reasonable grounds**, that:
- A person is committing or planning money laundering or terrorism financing
- A customer is not who they claim to be
- You have information relevant to the investigation of an offence

**Key SMR Rules:**
- Submit a **separate SMR each time** you form a new suspicion
- SMR obligations apply even if you **decide not to provide** the service
- You do NOT need to be certain â€” **suspicion** on reasonable grounds is sufficient
- **Even if you divert the customer to a non-cash payment method, you must still file an SMR if you hold a suspicion**

**Timeframes:**

| Scenario | Deadline |
|---|---|
| Suspicion relates to **terrorism financing** | Within **24 hours** |
| All other suspicions | Within **3 business days** |

**How to File:** Via [AUSTRAC Online](https://online.austrac.gov.au)

**Jeweller Example (from AUSTRAC):**
> A customer wants to buy $50,000 worth of loose diamonds in cash. Their low income doesn't support the purchase, and they can't provide evidence of dividend income. The compliance officer files an SMR. The senior manager rejects the transaction as it falls outside the business's risk appetite, telling the customer the cash amount exceeds accepted limits.

#### 7.10.2 Threshold Transaction Reports (TTRs)

**When You Must File:**
A TTR is required when you receive **$10,000 or more in physical currency** in a single transaction.

**Physical currency** = banknotes and coins. It does NOT include electronic transfers, cheques, credit/debit card payments, or cryptocurrency.

**Timeframe:** Within **10 business days** after the transaction.

**How to File:** Via [AUSTRAC Online](https://online.austrac.gov.au)

**Structuring Warning:**
If a customer appears to be **breaking up cash payments** to stay under $10,000, this is **structuring** â€” a criminal offence. This should also trigger an SMR.

#### 7.10.3 Annual Compliance Reports

> **ðŸ“Ž AUSTRAC Source:** [Step 2: Use Your Jeweller Program](https://www.austrac.gov.au/reforms/sector-specific-guidance/dealers-precious-metals-stones-and-products-guidance/jeweller-program-starter-kit/step-2-use-your-jeweller-program)

An annual compliance report submitted to AUSTRAC about how you met your obligations during the previous calendar year.

#### 7.10.4 Tipping-Off Prohibition

**Critical Legal Requirement:**

You **MUST NOT** disclose ("tip off") to the customer or any other person that:
- You have filed or intend to file an SMR
- You suspect or have formed a suspicion about the customer
- AUSTRAC is investigating or may investigate the customer

**Penalties for tipping off are severe** â€” this is a criminal offence.

**What You CAN Do:**
- Discuss your concerns with your compliance officer
- Seek legal advice
- Continue to provide the service normally (unless declining)
- File the SMR
- Tell the customer the refusal is because the transaction value exceeds accepted limits (as in AUSTRAC's example)

**What You CANNOT Do:**
- Tell the customer you've reported them
- Warn the customer that their transaction looks suspicious
- Discuss SMR details with colleagues who don't need to know

### 7.17 Real-World Customer Examples (Low / Medium / High Risk) â€” NEW PAGE

#### Example 1: Low-Risk Customer

**Scenario:** A jeweller accepts a request to create a custom diamond ring valued at **$12,000**. Customer asks to pay in cash.

**Key Points:**
- Customer provides reason: engagement ring
- Completes onboarding form, provides driver's licence
- No medium or high-risk factors identified â†’ rated **LOW risk**
- Simplified CDD: identity verification, sanctions check, PEP check
- Uses escalation checklist â€” no triggers present
- Transaction completed, business relationship concludes â€” no further ongoing CDD required
- **Compliance boils down to ~3 forms** for this typical customer

#### Example 2: Medium-Risk Customer

**Scenario:** Individual wants to purchase a custom bracelet and watch valued at **$15,000** with cash. Both contain precious stones. Individual is acting as representative for their employer (a sole trader).

**Key Points:**
- **Medium risk factors:** Employer is a domestic PEP (parent of a state MP) + representative is a third-party agent not enrolled with AUSTRAC
- Representative completes onboarding form on behalf of employer (customer)
- Identity verified for both representative AND customer (employer)
- PEP check: customer is family member of domestic PEP; representative is not a PEP
- Sanctions check completed â€” no matches
- Escalation triggers reviewed â€” no escalation needed
- Final rating remains **MEDIUM**
- Transaction completed, relationship concludes â€” no further ongoing CDD

#### Example 3: High-Risk Customer

**Scenario:** Individual wants to purchase **100-point loose diamonds** using **$50,000 in cash**.

**Key Points:**
- **High risk factors:** Cash transaction $50,000+ ; purchasing easily transportable high-value items (loose diamonds that retain value and don't need labour to improve)
- Must **escalate to AML/CTF compliance officer**
- Compliance officer conducts enhanced CDD:
  - Identity verification
  - **Source of funds check:** Customer claims dividends from shares
  - **Source of wealth check:** Customer claims salary and dividends
  - Customer's **low income doesn't support the purchase**
  - Customer **cannot provide dividend statements** â€” makes excuses
  - **Adverse media check** â€” no adverse results found
- Compliance officer forms **reasonable grounds for suspicion** â†’ submits **SMR to AUSTRAC**
- Senior manager **rejects the transaction** â€” falls outside business risk appetite
- Customer told refusal is because cash amount exceeds accepted limits
- Customer advised they can deposit funds into a bank account and pay via bank transfer or EFTPOS

### 7.19 FAQ â€” EXPANDED

| # | Question | Short Answer |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | I'm a jeweller who only accepts credit cards and bank transfers. Am I regulated? | No. If you never accept physical currency or virtual assets, you are not providing a designated service (Option 1). |
| 2 | I sometimes accept small cash payments under $10,000. Am I regulated? | Potentially. You must monitor for linked or structured transactions that cumulatively reach $10,000+. If they do, you need an AML/CTF program. |
| 3 | What if a customer pays $5,000 in cash today and $5,000 in cash next week for the same purchase? | These are likely linked transactions. Once they exceed $10,000 cumulatively, you are providing a designated service and must complete CDD. |
| 4 | Can I avoid regulation by telling customers to pay by bank transfer? | Yes, for legitimate customers. But if you already suspect criminal activity, redirecting to bank transfer does NOT remove your SMR obligation and could make you complicit. |
| 5 | Do watches count as precious products? | Yes, if they contain precious metals or stones. A stainless-steel watch with diamonds set on the face is a precious product. |
| 6 | Is a gold-plated item a precious product? | Only if it contains precious metals or stones at or above the defined thresholds (2%+ alloy weight). |
| 7 | What about costume jewellery? | Costume jewellery that contains NO precious metals or stones is NOT a precious product and is NOT regulated. |
| 8 | I'm a bullion dealer. Does this guidance apply to me? | No. Bullion dealing is a separate designated service with its own requirements. |
| 9 | What if I suspect money laundering but I'm not sure? | You don't need to be certain. If you have suspicion on reasonable grounds, file an SMR. |
| 10 | What are the penalties for non-compliance? | Non-compliance carries significant penalties. AUSTRAC will focus enforcement on entities that fail to enrol or make no meaningful effort. |
| 11 | How much does it cost to enrol with AUSTRAC? | Enrolment with AUSTRAC is free. |
| 12 | A customer wants to buy $50,000 worth of loose diamonds with cash. What's my risk rating for this customer? | Automatic **HIGH risk** â€” $50,000+ in physical currency is a high-risk factor. Escalate to compliance officer. |
| 13 | Are scrap metal dealers always high risk? | AUSTRAC identifies scrap metal dealers as a high-risk customer type because it's difficult to trace the origin of scrap jewellery. |
| 14 | Do I need to screen every cash customer for sanctions? | Yes â€” PEP and TFS screening is part of initial CDD for every customer in a regulated transaction. |
| 15 | Can I outsource my AML/CTF program? | You can use external consultants and appoint an external compliance officer, but you remain ultimately responsible. |
